Anagha Joshi

I am an ML researcher and software engineer with over eight years of experience building large-scale applications and practical AI systems. Previously, I was a graduate research assistant at the Institute of Artificial Intelligence at the department of Computer Science at University of Georgia, Athens, USA. For my masters thesis at UGA, I focused on Interpretable AI for Image Classification and I was advised by Dr. Jaewoo Lee. My research interests lie at the intersection of machine learning and software engineering (SE), with a focus on robustness, explainability, and symbolic–neural integration. My research background and industry experience in SE and explainable AI have shown me that the biggest bottleneck to AI adoption is often a lack of trustworthy and secure tools.

I previously worked as a Software Development Engineer at Amazon, where I designed scalable APIs, automated complex systems, and wrote clear technical documentation. I am now expanding my research into projects that connect my industry background with real-world impact, especially in explainable AI and AI systems that help organizations make better decisions with limited resources. I currently work with Omdena on research exploring how LLM-based systems can provide operationally safe and auditable recommendations for incident remediation in production environments—addressing hallucination risk through rigorous evaluation frameworks.

My goal is to help build technology that is robust, understandable, and accessible — whether that means designing multi-agent AI systems, deploying scalable APIs, or improving trust in AI-powered recommendations for everyday businesses.

Research

My current research focuses on LLM Evaluation for Incident Remediation. I am developing rigorous evaluation frameworks to assess how reliably language models can provide actionable guidance in high-stakes operational settings. This work directly addresses the challenge of building operationally safe, auditable AI systems—a central concern in production environments where hallucinations and unfounded recommendations can have serious consequences.

My broader research bridges software engineering and practical AI. I have developed AI-driven program analysis tools using Boolean tensor models for syntax error repair on real-world datasets. During my master's, I designed TPNet, an interpretable transformer-based image classifier that shows how deep learning models can remain transparent and auditable. Both research threads explore the same fundamental question: Can you trust what the model says? Whether evaluating incident remediation advice or understanding image classifier predictions, the core challenge is building mechanisms for robust, auditable AI.

I am motivated by research that tackles real-world challenges at the intersection of technology, environment, and human well-being. I am interested in building AI systems that stay understandable and make a measurable difference where they are needed most.
